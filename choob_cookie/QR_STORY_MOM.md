### A Legacy Passed Down by My Mom (Mana's Grandma Zahra)

I grew up in a small but bustling home in Tehran, watching my mom — my grandma — move between the kitchen and the courtyard, always doing something magical. She was a baker, an event planner, a homemaker, and a decorator for brides and bridal parties. But her true magic lived in a small tin box she kept on the counter.

Every afternoon, she'd pull out that tin and mix flour, sugar, and a secret blend of spices I wasn't allowed to know. She'd roll the dough into thin logs, slice them into perfect rounds, and sprinkle black and white sesame seeds on top. The aroma would drift through the house, and soon enough, neighbors and relatives would show up at our door, not just for a visit, but for a parting gift — a paper bag with her special tea cookies.

Those cookies were her love language. Every bite was her way of saying, "I care about you. Take a piece of my heart with you."

When we moved to America, that tin came with us. She kept baking, and I kept watching, learning the movements by heart. After she passed, I found myself standing in the same kitchen, doing the same things — mixing, rolling, sprinkling. The recipe was hers, but the love in it became mine.

Now I make them for my own family, and for the neighbors who remind me every time: "You should sell these — they're that good." Maybe someday. But for now, I just want to share a piece of our story with you.

These cookies are more than a snack — they're a hug from my kitchen to yours.
